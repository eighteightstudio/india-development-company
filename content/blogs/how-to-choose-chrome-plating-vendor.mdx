Selecting a hard chrome plating vendor is not a decision that should be made on price alone. A substandard chrome coat — whether too thin, incorrectly hardened, or poorly adhered — can cause component failure, hydraulic leakage, or extrusion line downtime. The cost of a rework far exceeds any savings made on a cheaper plating quote.

This guide is written for procurement managers, plant engineers, and factory owners evaluating chrome plating vendors in India. Use this checklist to make a decision you can stand behind.

## 1. Verify Years of Experience in Electroplating

Chrome plating is as much craft as it is chemistry. The behaviour of a chromic acid bath changes with temperature, contaminant load, additive depletion, and electrical parameters. An experienced plater reads these variables intuitively — a new operator relies on trial and error.

**What to ask:**
- How many years has the vendor been operating an electroplating facility?
- Have they worked with components similar to yours (piston rods, extrusion screws, rollers)?
- Can they provide references from clients in your industry?

A vendor with 10+ years of experience is a safer choice. A vendor with 30+ years — like India Development Company, established in 1992 — has seen and solved virtually every chrome plating challenge.

## 2. Evaluate Rectifier Capacity and Equipment Quality

The rectifier is the power source of the electroplating line. Its capacity (measured in amperes) determines how much current can be delivered to the bath — which directly affects plating speed, uniformity, and the ability to handle large or complex components.

**What to look for:**
- Rectifier capacity of at least 1000A for general industrial work
- 3000–5000A capacity for high-volume or large-component plating
- Regular maintenance and calibration records for all electrical equipment

India Development Company operates a **4000-ampere rectifier**, enabling high-volume production runs with consistent plating quality across large batches.

## 3. Confirm Hardness Specifications and Measurement

The defining property of hard chrome plating is its hardness — typically targeted at 55–70 RC depending on the application. For most industrial components (piston rods, cylinders, rollers, extrusion tooling), a hardness of **55–60 RC** is the industry standard.

A reputable vendor should:

- Specify the hardness range they achieve as a standard output
- Measure and document hardness using a calibrated Rockwell hardness tester
- Be willing to provide test certificates or inspection records

**Red flags:** A vendor who cannot specify the hardness of their chrome coat, or who has no measurement equipment on site, is not operating a professional facility.

## 4. Ask About Plating Thickness Range and Control

Chrome plating thickness is specified in micrometres (µm). The correct thickness depends on your application:

| Application | Typical Thickness |
|---|---|
| New coat — fine tolerance parts | 5–50 µm |
| General industrial components | 50–150 µm |
| Heavy repair coat | 150–500 µm |

A professional vendor should be able to plate to your specified thickness and verify it using appropriate measurement tools (eddy current or magnetic induction gauges).

IDC's standard thickness range is **5–500 µm** — covering the full spectrum from fine new coats to heavy repair plating.

## 5. Understand Their Plating Technique

Not all electroplating processes are the same. For hard chrome plating, the **Low Voltage High Ampere** technique is widely regarded as producing superior coating quality — with better adhesion, more uniform deposition, and optimal hardness development.

Ask your vendor:
- What voltage and current density do they operate at?
- Do they use a controlled, documented process — or is it operator-dependent?
- How do they manage bath chemistry (chromic acid concentration, trivalent chrome levels, temperature)?

IDC operates at **5–12 HV** using the Low Voltage High Ampere method — a deliberate choice for quality and consistency.

## 6. Check Turnaround Time and Batch Capacity

In a manufacturing environment, component availability directly affects production uptime. A chrome plating vendor who takes six weeks to return a critical piston rod creates a bottleneck — regardless of coating quality.

**Questions to ask:**
- What is the standard turnaround time for components of your size and type?
- Can they handle your volume — both current and anticipated future requirements?
- Do they have capacity to take urgent or priority jobs?

## 7. Assess Safety and Regulatory Compliance

Electroplating facilities handle hazardous chemicals — hexavalent chromium compounds, acids, and heavy metal solutions. A vendor who does not operate a safe facility is a liability risk for your supply chain.

**Safety indicators to look for:**
- Proper personal protective equipment (PPE) for all workers: acid-proof clothing, gloves, boots, helmets
- Adequate ventilation and fume extraction in the plating area
- Chemical storage and disposal practices that meet regulatory standards
- Evidence of compliance with environmental and industrial safety regulations

At IDC, all workers are equipped with acid-proof clothing, protective gloves, boots, and helmets. Chemical solutions are maintained and disposed of in compliance with applicable regulations.

## 8. Evaluate Communication and Responsiveness

A chrome plating vendor is a production partner, not just a commodity supplier. The ability to communicate clearly — about specifications, timelines, problems, and resolutions — is a practical requirement.

During your evaluation, assess:
- How quickly do they respond to inquiries?
- Are they able to discuss technical specifications clearly?
- Do they proactively flag issues with components brought in for repair?
